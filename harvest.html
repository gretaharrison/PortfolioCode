HARVEST

About The Project

For this assignment, we used design-thinking for the greater good. My team's topic was Factory Farming and our task was to build a project that creates positive social change in communities and demonstrates the value of design thinking... all within a 2 week timeframe...

Our Solution...

We Built A Store

This project was all about reminding ourselves that UX is bigger than purely digital design and we were encouraged to think outside of the box. We developed a conceptual grocery store experience where customers can hand pick their produce directly from the source in an urban rooftop setting. We named the store Harvest.

An Interactive Desktop Experience

To display our design thinking we created an interactive website prototype for our conceptual store. We used the site to showcase how Harvest tackles the environmental issues that are a result of Factory Farming, and also enhances the shopping experience for consumers by creating a deeper emotional tie with their food.

And Now Learn About How We Got There...

The Challenge: Designing for Social Impact

Factory Farming: Over the last two decades, small- and medium-scale agricultural farms have given way to massive factory farms that export their merchandise all around the world. The number of large-scale farming operations continues to grow, creating more waste and impacting the environment negatively.

Concept - General Assembly Class Project

The Goal

To use the power of human-centered design to effectuate social change. Designers must recognize the challenges around implementation and deliver comprehensive prototypes (solutions) with clear implementation plans.

The Dream Team

For this project, I was lucky enough of working with Alyse Gilbert, Evangeline Hsiao and Mike Tseng. This is the first time we would be working with one another.

My Role

Ideation, Market Research, Personas, User Flow, Map of Harvest, Low-Fidelity Wires, High Fidelity Prototype, Movie

Introduction

Designing for social impact is about looking into the future and finding tangible solutions for change. Less bad is not good enough and thinking critically about implementable solutions was an essential part of our ideation process. Creating Harvest allowed me to see the true impact of design by broadening the context far beyond the digital interface. Instead of focusing on just user and stakeholder needs, we had to dive deep into the root behavioral problems of our society - not an easy task - and try to envision the future: 5 years, 10 years, 50 years down the line.


As Alex Steffen of Ideo says, “Understanding that kind of change requires not just a rational inquiry into the facts, but also an intuitive engagement with what the future feels like.”


My teammates and I approached this challenge with enthusiasm, and I’m really proud to display our entire process in this case study.

Affinity Mapping

Initial Ideation

In order to develop our MVP, our first course of action was to narrow down what sub-sector within Factory Farming we wanted to focus on. We went to the whiteboard and began sketching an affinity map with Factory Farming at the epicenter. We discovered that “Factory Farming” is far more complex and widespread than we initially realized. From environmental distress and health issues, to animal welfare and food safety, the amount of challenges stemming from Factory Farming left us in disbelief and eager to begin designing for solutions.

Refined Map, Focused on Waste

From here we conducted some initial research to understand the landscape of these various challenges in more detail. As a team we reconvened and decided that Industrial Agriculture was where we felt we could achieve the most impact. We created a second affinity map to see what specific challenges exist within the sub-sector of Waste.

Analysis of Current System

We created a visual analysis that broke down the traditional model that exists today.

User Research

Before we dove deeper into research and ideation, we needed to examine current user behavior. We created a survey that focused on gaining insight on user behaviors and opinions towards shopping at farmer’s markets. The goal was to understand the motives behind shopping at a farmer’s markets, and to get a sense what users might look for in an even more progressive grocery store experience.

Personas

Based off of heuristic evaluation, research and the demographics from our survey, we came up with three unique personas

Field Study

Taking a break from our research and ideation, we conducted a field trip to a couple local urban farms. We visited Lazybone, a boutique/garden in Santa Monia, and The Cook’s Garden, a true urban farm in the heart of Venice.

Comparative Competitive Analysis

Now that we had an understanding of the current model, our user’s needs and some some insight from local farmers, it was time to examine comparative urban farms. We spent a full day researching, and came across two key players: The Farmery and Lufa Farms.


Like our concept for Harvest, The Farmery is a conceptual idea for an urban farm grocery experience. Lufa Farms, on the other hand, is the first commercial rooftop greenhouse. We deconstructed both, and took a closer look at how their business models drive solutions to various aspects of waste and education.

Technological Feasibility

Determining the feasilibity and scalability of our idea was an extremely important part of our design process. We knew that in order to inspire change, our design solution had to be at least partly implementable. Through researching advances in planting technology we came across hydroponic and aeroponic planting towers. These towers allow farmer to grow up instead of out - perfect for rooftop environments that are limited on square footage, but not air space.


But is it scalable? According to research we’ve done and speaking with resellers of the tower, what you can grow in 43,560 square feet (or one acre of land), you can grow in only 4,000 square feet with the tower gardens. One tower can grow anywhere from 20–44 produce items and if you are using a tower with a capability of growing 44 plants and had 40 towers, you theoretically could grow up to 1,760 different plants. Growing vertically allows you to grow so much more compared to growing out. This would make sense considering the already limited space in metropolitan areas.

Ideation

Inspired by the urban farms we visited as well as researching existing technologies, we knew we had the potential to create an unique experience. What if we could grow everything in-house and what if we could allow our shoppers to handpick their own produce if they choose to? This sparked our imagination to think outside the box.

Mission & Tagline

Harvest as a Solution

Harvest not only tackles the environmental issues caused by Factory Farming, but it also changes the shopping experience for consumers by creating a deeper emotional tie with their food. To make sure we were keeping the user in mind throughout our design process, we created a chart to track and differentiate between Design Solving and UX for consumers.

User Journey

To display our user flow, we created an interactive map. We took into considerations of Alice’s need for fresh fruit in the Apple and Citrus Grove, Joaquin’s need for fresh herbs in the Herb Garden, and Elizabeth’s need for a fun educational experience in our Test Kitchen.

Sketching

We went to the whiteboard walls to brainstorm and sketch our first iteration of Harvest’s website.

Low-Fidelity Wireframes

Our next step was to translate the initial sketches into digital renderings. We made low fidelity wireframes using Sketch. We printed these wireframes to create a paper prototype to test with users.

High Fidelity Prototype

After testing our low fidelity wires and making a few iterations, we were able to start creating our high-fidelity prototype in Sketch. Our design aesthetic was intended to feel clean, modern and approachable with a clear representation of our mission statement site-wide. To thoughtfully develop the look and feel of our website we created a style guide that included a custom designed logo, color schemes, typography guidelines, image assets and more. This allowed the entire team to contribute to the final prototype.
