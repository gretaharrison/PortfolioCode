PERFORMANCE BIKE

The Challenge

Re-imagine the website’s existing user flow for purchasing a complete mountain bike. Provide a streamline and simple solution with the existing business in mind.

Goal

Give the entire site a facelift by improving navigability and re-vamping the user experience from home page through checkout.

Concept - General Assembly Class Project

Tools

- - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -

User Research

In order to get user data in such a tight timeframe, I needed to create a survey and send it out right away. To come up with questions, I did a my Task Analysis on Performance Bike’s website, focusing my analysis on current features and how they affect the user flow. My goal with the User Survey was to obtain the demographics, cycling experience and online shopping habits of my target user-base. I also hoped that a few participants would be willing to allow me to question them further

In-Depth User Interview

Luckily I had a few participants who were willing to conduct a follow up interview. Instead of just asking questions, I planned a Contextual Inquiry that centered around the interviewee navigating through the existing Performance Bike website. I gave them a specific task before starting (to purchase a complete mountain bike), and made sure to record our conversation using my iPhone’s Voice Memo app. My goal for this interview was to determine the user’s primary needs and pain points surrounding the existing flow. The interview took a total of 9 minutes, and by the end, I had a good understanding of their needs and pain points.

  • Major Needs: to purchase a bike, to have it assembled, and to be able to sort by things like gender early on in the shopping process.
  • Major Pain Points: Cryptic bike terminology that they did not understand, an avalanche of categories on the product listing page, and difficult navigation throughout the site - including the use of the back button after getting stuck multiple times.

Persona

From the quantitative data I received from my survey and the qualitative data I received from my user interview, I was able to create my target Persona and Scenario:


Alice wants to buy a mountain bike. She has a budget of $500-$1000. She has been to a few local bike stores, but is attracted to Performance Bike’s website because of their large selection and low prices. She is looking for a highly rated bike that is competitively priced, and requires no assembly.

Information Architecture

Now that I had an idea on what elements I wanted to target in my design, I re-visited the initial scenario and imagined how my user would go through it, accounting for every decision he/she may have to make. After creating my site map, I used my scenario to create a re-imagined User Flow.

Researching Best Practices

To understand what specific improvements to make, I needed to break down the problem into key elements and determine what features on the site should be improved and why. To learn about best practices, I headed over to the Nielsen Norman Group website and read articles on data validated best practices for E-Commerce sites like Performance Bike. Some good and bad practices that could be directly applied to my re-design included:

  • Get rid of all items that look like banner ads. Because of “banner blindness” eye mapping tests now show that we ignore images that are shaped like banners because we associate them with ads.
  • Get rid of stock photos or photos that look like stock photos. Even if they are just meant to make a site prettier or to jazz it up, focus on only showing images that are relevant
  • Make reviews or the review section of the product page more robust. People like reviews and get a lot of information about the product from them. If you are able to learn more about the user, sort reviews and summarize reviews - the user will be happy.
  • Make sure that users get feedback when they add an item to their cart. Either direct them to the cart page or use a modal.

Sketching

I created hand drawn sketches after my user flow. I focused on not including any features that were not in my user flow to make sure it was as simple and intuitive as possible.


Wireframes

After sketching, I transformed my sketches into low-fidelity wireframes using Sketch. I ended up creating 18 wireframes total - documenting the flow from entry at the Home Page through checkout.


Clickable Prototype

After my wires were complete, I uploaded them to InVision and created a clickable prototype going through my current flow of purchasing a complete mountain bike.
I was able to use my clickable prototype to test my re-designed user flow and made iterations based on user’s comments.

Some of the iterations I made were removing a side bar on the Product Listing Page, making the Review Section larger, adding a PayPal option at checkout, and summarizing my product descriptions to just a few sentences worth of text.
